Abstract

The Constitution (One Hundred and Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Bill, 2024 is a serious and overdue attempt to reduce the financial and governance cost of India's near-continuous election cycle. But the Bill, as drafted, carries a structural flaw — the remainder term provision — which will, in practice, defeat its own purpose. When a House is dissolved mid-cycle and reconstituted only for the unexpired remainder of the synchronized term, the result is a government without the mandate, stability, or time-horizon to govern effectively. The instability ONOE was designed to eliminate is preserved under a different name.

This paper proposes a legislative alternative: a Constitutional Bridge Mechanism, enacted as Article 82B of the Constitution, together with a companion judicial safeguard, Article 82C. When a government falls through a no-confidence motion under Article 75(3) or Article 164 and no alternative majority can be constituted within fourteen days, the President or Governor assumes the functions of Head of Government in addition to Head of State on a strictly limited, temporary basis, within the constitutionally prescribed council-size ceiling. Ministerial portfolios — excluding Home Affairs, Defence, and Intelligence, which vest directly in the President or Governor — are filled on the recommendation of the party or alliance holding the largest number of seats, the Recommending Party. A dedicated anti-defection lock prevents horse-trading during the bridge period, a tie-break rule governs the rare case of equal seat shares, the Speaker's continuity is preserved with a fallback election and interim chair, and an expedited Judicial Review Committee under Article 82C gives any aggrieved minority of members fast-tracked constitutional recourse without displacing the ordinary jurisdiction of the courts. The bridge terminates automatically upon proof of majority. The synchronized election cycle is preserved intact throughout.

This is a revised edition of the original proposal. It restructures the mechanism to close three gaps identified in the first draft — control over ministerial appointments, the incentive for horse-trading during a prolonged bridge, and the absence of a judicial safeguard — and expresses the complete, amended text of Article 82B and the new Article 82C in full legislative drafting language.

Introduction

Right diagnosis, wrong remedy — and how a bridge, not a shorter term, closes the gap.

When I look at the 129th Constitutional Amendment Bill, I see something that is both right and wrong at the same time. It is right in its diagnosis: India spends enormous resources, administrative energy, and political attention on elections that happen somewhere in the country almost every year. The Model Code of Conduct freezes governance. Coalition governments collapse mid-term. The idea of synchronizing all elections to a single cycle is not new — it was the practice until 1967, and its erosion was the result of political fragmentation rather than constitutional design.

But the Bill is wrong in one specific and consequential way. When it provides that a House dissolved before completing its term shall be reconstituted only for the unexpired remainder of that term, it creates the very problem it was designed to solve. A government elected for eighteen months does not govern with the confidence of a government with a five-year mandate. It governs defensively, with one eye on the next election. The remainder term is not a solution to instability. It is instability with a different label.

What this paper proposes is a repair of that flaw. The Constitutional Bridge Mechanism, enacted as Article 82B, ensures that when a government falls and no alternative majority can be immediately constituted, governance continues through a temporary, constitutionally limited arrangement — without dissolving the House, without holding mid-term elections, and without creating a remainder-term government. This revised edition closes three additional gaps: it fixes exactly who controls what during ministerial appointment, it shuts the door on horse-trading for as long as the bridge lasts, and it adds a fast-track judicial safeguard, Article 82C, so the mechanism is never unaccountable even for a single day. The synchronized cycle is preserved intact.

Background: The 129th Amendment and the Remainder Term Problem

A shorter term is not the absence of instability. It is instability, compressed.

2.1What the 129th Amendment Proposes

The Constitution (One Hundred and Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Bill, 2024, currently before the Joint Parliamentary Committee, proposes amendments to Articles 82A, 83, 172, and 327A to enable simultaneous elections for the Lok Sabha and all State Legislative Assemblies. A common synchronized election date is established and all Houses are expected to complete their terms in alignment with this date.

The critical provision is the one dealing with mid-term dissolution. The Bill proposes that if the Lok Sabha or a State Legislative Assembly is dissolved before completing its term, elections held to reconstitute that House shall be for the unexpired remainder of the term only.

2.2Why the Remainder Term is a Structural Flaw

✖Does not eliminate instability — it shortens it. A government with 18 months in office has the same structural incentives for short-termism and populism as a caretaker. The compression of instability into fewer months does not remove it.
✖Contradicts the democratic mandate. Under Article 75(3), the Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the House. A remainder-term government carries a mandate voters did not confer for the full period. This dilutes democratic legitimacy in a way the Constitution does not sanction.
✖Defeats the purpose of ONOE. Recurring political instability under an ONOE regime using remainder terms could produce more frequent elections than the current system — the opposite of the intended outcome.
Core Problem. The 129th Amendment assumes Houses will not collapse mid-cycle. When they do — and they will — the remainder term provision breaks synchronization. Article 82B ensures synchronization is maintained regardless of mid-cycle political events.

Constitutional Framework: Existing Provisions

Article 82B borrows no new power. It gives direct effect to power that already exists.

Art. 75(3)
& 164

No-Confidence and Ministerial Accountability

Article 75(3) provides that the Council of Ministers shall be collectively responsible to the House of the People. The established convention is that this collective responsibility is tested through a motion of no-confidence; when the House passes such a motion, the Council of Ministers is obligated to resign. Article 164 provides the equivalent for State Legislative Assemblies. The Constitutional Bridge Mechanism activates at precisely this moment.

Art. 74
& 163

Council of Ministers and Advice

Article 74(1) provides that there shall be a Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at the head to aid and advise the President, and that the President shall act in accordance with such advice — binding since the 44th Amendment. Article 163(1) provides the equivalent for states. During the bridge period, this relationship is temporarily modified: the Head of Government function vests directly in the President or Governor, so the advice mechanism operates internally within the same constitutional office.

Art. 75(1A)
& 164(1A)

Ceiling on Council Size

Inserted by the Ninety-First Amendment, these clauses cap the total strength of the Council of Ministers, including the Prime Minister or Chief Minister, at fifteen per centum of the total membership of the House, with a floor of twelve Ministers for a State Legislative Assembly. Article 82B(3)(a) binds the President or Governor to this existing ceiling — the bridge mechanism does not create a new, uncapped executive.

Art. 83
& 172

Duration of Houses

Article 83(2) provides that the House of the People, unless sooner dissolved, shall continue for five years from the date appointed for its first meeting; Article 172(1) provides the equivalent for State Legislative Assemblies. The bridge mechanism rests on a critical insight: there is no constitutional requirement that dissolution follow immediately upon the fall of a government. The House continues to exist. Its members remain in office. Article 82B uses this continuity to keep governance functioning until a majority re-emerges.

Art. 93
& 178

The Speaker — Continuity and Reconstitution

Under Articles 94 and 179, the Speaker vacates office on ceasing to be a member of the House, but otherwise continues in office — including after a no-confidence motion against the government. The Speaker is not a member of the Council of Ministers and is unaffected by its fall. This continuity is central to the mechanism and is addressed fully in Section 4.5.

Art. 356

Why It Does Not Apply Here

Article 356 is an emergency provision invoked when the government of a State cannot be carried on in accordance with the Constitution, typically on the Governor's report at the Centre's instance. S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) circumscribed its use severely. Article 82B has no connection with it: it is triggered by a constitutionally objective fact — the passage of a no-confidence motion — not Central initiative or a Governor's report. No legislative body is suspended and no Central interference in state governance occurs. Article 82B expressly disclaims any invocation of Article 356.

The Constitutional Bridge Mechanism — Full Proposal

Ten components, each grounded in a specific constitutional provision, each with a precise operational scope.

4.1Triggering Conditions

Two conditions must be simultaneously satisfied for the bridge to commence, automatically and without proclamation:

Condition One. A motion of no-confidence has been passed against the Council of Ministers under Article 75(3) (Lok Sabha) or Article 164 (State Legislative Assembly), and the outgoing Council has tendered its resignation.

Condition Two. No member or combination of members has demonstrated, through a letter of support signed by a majority of members, the ability to form an alternative government within fourteen days of acceptance of that resignation.

4.2The HOG–HOS Merger and its Constitutional Basis

During the bridge period, the President of India — for the Lok Sabha — and the Governor of the concerned State — for a State Legislative Assembly — assume the functions of Head of Government in addition to Head of State. The constitutional basis derives from Articles 52–61 (President) and 153–161 (Governor). Both offices already vest executive power of the Union and of the State respectively. The merger is not the creation of new power; it is direct exercise of power that already constitutionally vests in these offices, temporarily without the mediation of a Council of Ministers that does not exist.

Operational Limits on HOG. Limited to (1) maintenance of existing policy, (2) continuance of existing programmes and schemes, (3) administrative functions necessary for normal government functioning. No new policy, no new scheme, and no financial commitment beyond limits prescribed by Parliament — except with House approval.

4.3The Recommending Party System & Council-Size Ceiling

The party or pre-poll alliance holding the largest number of seats — the Recommending Party — is certified by the Speaker. Two separate powers are then exercised by two separate actors, and this revision draws that line precisely:

✔President/Governor controls: the total number of ministers (bound by the existing 15%-of-House ceiling under Article 75(1A) / 164(1A)), and which portfolios exist.
✔Recommending Party controls: which of its own nominated members fills which of those specified portfolios.

This split answers the objection that the President or Governor would be "choosing the government." They are not. They set the shape of the table; the largest party decides who sits where at it.

4.4Reserved Portfolios — Home, Defence & Intelligence

The Ministry of Home Affairs, the Ministry of Defence, and all organs of intelligence and internal security — or their State equivalents — are not included among the portfolios offered to the Recommending Party. They vest directly in, and are administered solely by, the President or the Governor throughout the bridge period.

Why. If coercive or security powers were available to a political party simultaneously negotiating coalition formation, those powers could be turned toward influencing negotiations, security deployments, or the political process itself. The original draft reserved only Home Affairs; this revision extends the same logic to Defence and Intelligence, since both carry an equal or greater capacity to be misused as leverage during a period of live political bargaining.

4.5The Speaker During the Bridge Period

The Speaker is constitutionally distinct from the Council of Ministers and unaffected by its fall. Under Articles 94 and 179, a no-confidence motion has no effect on Speaker's tenure — the Speaker continues to hold office and discharge all constitutional functions, including certifying the Recommending Party as a factual, non-discretionary determination of House composition.

If the Speaker's own office falls vacant during the bridge period, the House elects a new Speaker by simple majority of members present and voting, who holds office for the remainder of the bridge period and stands down when it ends, whereupon the ordinary Article 93 / 178 procedure resumes. If that election ends in a tie, a fresh election is held after an interval fixed by the rules of procedure. In the intervening period — between the vacancy and a valid election — the senior-most officer of the House Secretariat presides for the sole purpose of conducting that election, exercising no other function of the Speaker's office.

4.6HOG Accountability to the House

The President or Governor acting as Head of Government is fully accountable to the House in the same manner as a Prime Minister or Chief Minister — Question Hour, calling attention motions, adjournment motions, and short duration discussions all apply with full force. The bridge period is a parliamentary arrangement, not a presidential one; the HOG is never shielded from oversight by virtue of holding constitutional office.

4.7Accountability During the Bridge Period

✔Legislative accountability. The House sits and functions normally; Ministers may be questioned, their decisions debated.
✔Opposition status. All parties and members outside the Recommending Party are constitutionally treated as the opposition, with full rights and procedural protections.
✔Limitations on HOG. A Minister may not be unilaterally removed by the President or Governor — removal requires a House resolution, moved at the President's or Governor's instance and passed by majority.
✔Judicial review. Every action during the bridge period remains subject to the ordinary jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and High Courts — and, under this revision, to the expedited Article 82C committee as well. See Section 5.

4.8Bridge Termination

The bridge is self-terminating. It ends automatically upon Proof of Majority — any combination of members submitting a signed letter of support of a House majority, upon which the President or Governor forthwith invites that leader to form government — or upon Expiry of House Term under Articles 83 / 172, at which point the synchronized election proceeds in the normal course. There is no periodic floor test and no artificial deadline; the mechanism remains open to majority formation at all times.

4.9Anti-Defection Lock & Prohibition on Horse-Trading New

The 4.8 mechanism — "the bridge ends the moment a majority forms" — only holds together if majority formation cannot be gamed through defection and re-alignment while the bridge is running. This revision closes that gap directly.

No new alliances. Once the Recommending Party is first certified, no party or combination of members may form a new political alliance for the purpose of constituting or displacing it during the bridge period. A member of a party outside the Recommending Party gains no ministerial right merely by virtue of holding seats — being "in" a coalition confers no entitlement to actually run a ministry unless that party is the certified Recommending Party.

The 25% threshold. If members holding, in aggregate, less than 25% of the Recommending Party's own strength resign from it or from the House, the Recommending Party's status is unaffected and no Minister is removed on that account. If 25% or more resign, the Speaker re-certifies which party or alliance now holds the largest number of seats — but even then, re-certification alone does not remove a sitting Minister; removal still requires the House-resolution process under 4.7. This prevents both a trivial defection from destabilizing government and a large, genuine shift in numbers from being ignored.

Ordinary Tenth Schedule switching is suspended for this purpose. Article 82B, read with this clause, displaces the normal disqualification-for-defection framework during the bridge period and substitutes this bespoke threshold rule — because the normal framework was built for a functioning government-versus-opposition floor, not for a caretaker bridge where the incentive to defect is structurally different.

Because the door to defection and alliance-shopping is shut, a bridge that runs long is not a bridge that invites chaos — see the discussion of long-duration bridges in Section 8.

4.10Tied Recommending Party New

Where two or more parties or pre-poll alliances hold an equal and the largest number of seats, and are not themselves in alliance with each other, both jointly constitute the Recommending Party. For every specified portfolio, each must submit one nominee; if they cannot agree on a single name within five days, the Speaker — at the President's or Governor's request — opens candidature to the whole House, and the member with the most votes is appointed.

Accountability follows the same joint logic: if a Minister nominated by one of the tied parties is found to have acted wrongly, responsibility and any consequent action attaches equally to both parties constituting the Recommending Party, not only to the one that nominated the individual Minister.

Judicial Safeguard — The Expedited Review Committee New

A bridge with no judicial safety net is a bridge nobody should trust. Article 82C is that net.

Every version of this proposal has said that ordinary judicial review continues to apply during the bridge period. That is necessary but not sufficient — ordinary litigation timelines are not built for a mechanism designed to be temporary. This revision adds Article 82C, an expedited, self-contained review channel that runs alongside, and never instead of, the ordinary courts.

5.1How It Is Triggered

Not less than 5% of the total membership of the House of the People, or of a State Legislative Assembly, may jointly sign a request to the Chief Justice of India, or the Chief Justice of the relevant High Court, asking that a Judicial Review Committee be constituted to examine a specific resolution, guideline, order, or action taken under Article 82B.

5.2What It Does

On receipt of a valid request, the Chief Justice constitutes the Committee forthwith. The Committee examines the impugned action and issues whatever order it considers appropriate under the Constitution and the law — it is not an advisory body; its orders bind.

5.3What It Does Not Do

It does not replace, narrow, or gate the ordinary jurisdiction of the Supreme Court or High Courts. A person may bring a public interest litigation or any other ordinary proceeding entirely independently of Article 82C, at any time. The Committee is additive, not exclusive.

5.4Sunset

The Committee stands automatically dissolved the moment the bridge period ends — whether by proof of majority or by natural expiry — or the moment an election date is announced and finalised, whichever comes first. Orders already passed remain valid and binding after dissolution; nothing further is heard by the Committee itself. Anything left unresolved at that point reverts to the ordinary courts through the ordinary process. The Committee's entire reason for existing is speed during a short, defined window — not a parallel judiciary that outlives its trigger.

Early Dissolution and Synchronization Continuity

Voluntary exit can break the cycle just as surely as a no-confidence motion.

A separate but related question is what happens when a government seeks early dissolution on the advice of the Prime Minister or Chief Minister — not through a no-confidence motion but by choice. Under the ONOE framework, early dissolution would break synchronization just as surely as a no-confidence motion.

Article 82B therefore also provides that dissolution on the advice of the Head of Government, before expiry of the synchronized term, requires the concurrence of all other Legislatures under the ONOE framework. If any Legislature withholds concurrence, the request for early dissolution is denied — making mid-cycle dissolution practically very difficult, and ensuring the synchronized cycle is disrupted only with genuine, broad constitutional consensus.

In the last three months of the synchronized five-year term, the Election Commission of India shall fix a common election date under Article 324. If all Heads of Government reach consensus on a date, that date is adopted; if not, within thirty days of the commencement of the last three months, the Election Commission determines the common date independently.

ARTICLE 82C — Expedited Judicial Review During the Bridge Period New

(1)

Where not less than five per centum of the total membership of the House of the People, or of a Legislative Assembly of a State, as the case may be, consider any resolution, guideline, order, or action taken by the President, the Governor, a Minister, or the House under Article 82B during the continuance of a bridge period to be contrary to the Constitution or the law, such members may, by a request signed by them, apply to the Chief Justice of India or the Chief Justice of the High Court of the State concerned, as the case may be, for the constitution of a Judicial Review Committee.

(2)

On receipt of a request under clause (1), the Chief Justice of India or the Chief Justice of the High Court, as the case may be, shall constitute a Judicial Review Committee forthwith, which shall examine the resolution, guideline, order, or action complained of and pass such order as it considers appropriate in accordance with the Constitution and the law.

(3)

The jurisdiction of the Judicial Review Committee under this Article is supplementary to, and not in derogation of, the ordinary jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and the High Courts, including their jurisdiction to entertain a public interest litigation or any other proceeding independently of this Article.

(4)

The Judicial Review Committee constituted under this Article shall stand dissolved upon the termination of the bridge period under Article 82B, or upon the announcement and finalisation of the date of an election, whether under the ordinary constitutional process or otherwise, whichever is earlier. An order passed by the Committee prior to its dissolution shall remain valid and binding notwithstanding such dissolution, but no further hearing shall be conducted by the Committee after such dissolution.

(5)

Nothing in this Article shall prevent any person aggrieved by an order of the Judicial Review Committee, or by any matter not finally disposed of before the dissolution of the Committee, from seeking recourse before the Supreme Court or the High Court in accordance with the ordinary procedure of law.

Addressing Potential Objections

Every mechanism invites a "but what if" — here are the ones this proposal has to answer.

Q.Does this violate the basic structure doctrine?

The parliamentary character of the Constitution, identified in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) as basic structure, rests on executive accountability to the legislature. Article 82B does not eliminate this. The House continues to sit. Ministers remain accountable. The HOG itself is accountable under clause (6). The bridge is temporary and automatically terminates upon majority formation. Parliamentary character is preserved throughout.

Q.Is the HOG–HOS merger incompatible with the Constitution?

The merger is temporary, limited, and circumscribed by clause (2). The President and Governor already vest executive power under Articles 53 and 154. Article 82B gives direct effect to that existing vesting during a period when no Council of Ministers exists to mediate it. This is not Presidential government — it is a parliamentary bridge.

Q.Could the Recommending Party abuse its ministerial positions?

The structural constraints substantially limit this risk. Ministers cannot introduce new policy. Home Affairs, Defence and Intelligence are not available to them. They are subject to full parliamentary oversight and the removal procedure of clause (7). All decisions remain subject to judicial review under clause (11) and Article 82C.

Q.Does the ban on new alliances restrict members' political freedom to associate?

The restriction under clause (8) is narrow and time-bound: it prevents the formation of a new alliance for the specific purpose of constituting or displacing the Recommending Party during a defined, self-terminating window. It does not restrict a member's freedom to resign, to vote as they choose, or to associate for any other purpose. The alternative — permitting continuous re-alignment during the bridge — is precisely the horse-trading that would make a long bridge period untenable and would replicate the instability ONOE is meant to end.

Q.Is the tied Recommending Party rule fair to a party that narrowly missed being the sole largest?

The rule under clause (12) applies only where seat counts are genuinely equal, not merely close. Where they are equal, joint recommendation with equal accountability is the more defensible allocation than an arbitrary tie-breaking rule such as alphabetical order or lot, both of which would carry no democratic content at all.

Q.Does a Judicial Review Committee constituted by the Chief Justice intrude on judicial independence or create a parallel judiciary?

The Committee under Article 82C is constituted by the Chief Justice, from within the judiciary, exercising a docket-management function analogous to constituting any special bench — it is not an executive or legislative body reviewing itself. Clause (3) expressly preserves ordinary jurisdiction in parallel, and clause (4) sunsets the Committee the moment its trigger condition ends. It is closer to an expedited-hearing mechanism than to a new court.

Q.What if the bridge period lasts for years?

The self-terminating character of Article 82B means the bridge ends the moment any combination produces a majority. Political parties retain every incentive to form one — the Recommending Party to secure full government, the opposition to displace it from ministerial positions — and clause (8)'s anti-defection lock ensures that incentive is channelled into forming a genuine majority rather than into piecemeal defection that never quite crosses the threshold. At the latest, the bridge ends on natural expiry of the House term.

Q.Does this undermine federalism by giving the Governor too much power?

Article 82B is triggered by a constitutionally objective event — the passage of a no-confidence motion — not by Central government initiative. The Governor acts under the constraints of Article 82B itself, not on the advice of the Central government. Clause (11) expressly excludes any connection with Article 356. This is structurally different from Article 356, where the Governor acts as an agent of the Centre.

Honest Limitations

Two gaps from the first draft are closed. What remains open is stated plainly.

■

Draft text requires Parliamentary scrutiny

The text of Article 82B and Article 82C proposed in Section 7 is a working draft. It requires examination by the Parliamentary Standing Committee, the Law Commission, and constitutional experts before any definitive formulation is possible.

✔

Recommending Party definition in coalition contexts

Resolved in this revision by clause (12) — the tied Recommending Party rule now gives an explicit procedure for equal seat shares, including nominee agreement, House-vote fallback, and joint accountability.

✔

Relationship with the Tenth Schedule

Resolved in this revision by clauses (7)(b)–(c) and (8), and the extended non-obstante clause (13) — the ordinary defection framework is expressly displaced during the bridge period by the 25% threshold rule.

■

Are the numerical thresholds correctly calibrated?

The 25% defection threshold in clause (7), and the 5% membership threshold to trigger Article 82C in clause (1), are reasoned starting points, not empirically derived figures. They require calibration against India's actual party-size distribution and coalition history before enactment.

■

The interim Speaker's-office role needs Rules of Procedure

Clause (5)(c) assigns interim presiding functions to the senior-most Secretariat officer. The precise scope of that role — what, if anything, it may certify or decide — needs elaboration in the Rules of Procedure of each House, not in the constitutional text itself.

■

The mechanism has not been tested in practice

This is a proposal. Its practical operation across India's diverse political environments cannot be fully predicted without implementation experience. The paper claims constitutional coherence — not operational perfection.

Conclusion

A bridge, built with locks on both ends and a court on call.

One Nation One Election is a legitimate constitutional objective. The financial cost of elections, the governance paralysis caused by the Model Code of Conduct, and the political distortion produced by year-round electoral calculations are real problems that deserve a serious constitutional response. The 129th Amendment is a serious attempt at that response.

But the remainder term provision is a structural flaw that will, in time, break the synchronization ONOE is designed to establish. Political instability is not a hypothetical. Indian parliamentary history is full of governments that have fallen before the expiry of their terms. Every such collapse under the 129th Amendment as currently drafted produces a mid-cycle election, breaks synchronization, and creates a remainder-term government that cannot govern with the confidence and mandate of a full-term administration.

Article 82B, read with Article 82C, offers a constitutionally grounded alternative. It uses the existing constitutional office of the President and Governor, limits their executive functions strictly and within the existing council-size ceiling, preserves the legislative functions of the House, ensures democratic participation through the Recommending Party system, removes Home Affairs, Defence, and Intelligence from political reach, anchors the Speaker's role in the existing constitutional framework with a tie-break fallback, closes the door on horse-trading through a calibrated anti-defection lock, resolves the tied-seat scenario with joint accountability, holds the HOG to full parliamentary accountability, gives every aggrieved minority of members a fast-track judicial channel that never displaces the ordinary courts, and terminates automatically the moment democratic majority re-emerges.

The Constitution of India was built to be amended. It has been amended one hundred and six times. Each amendment has been an attempt to make the constitutional machinery work better for the people it governs. Article 82B and Article 82C are proposed in that spirit — not to alter the parliamentary character of the Constitution, but to give it the resilience it needs to absorb political instability without abandoning the synchronized democratic cycle that One Nation One Election seeks to establish.
